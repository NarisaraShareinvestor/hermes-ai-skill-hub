## 2.  2026 Outlook

## 2.1  Global Economic Outlook

The global economy in 2026 is projected to expand at a similar rate to 2025. This is expected to be driven by rapidly increasing investments in AI in North America and Asia, coupled with more accommodative fiscal and monetary policies in many countries, which will offset the impact of reciprocal tariffs and Trump's policy uncertainty. However, concerns about an AI bubble and close monitoring of geopolitical conflicts remain. The IMF in January 2026 expected global economic growth at 3.3% in 2026.

## 2.2  Global Petroleum and Petrochemical Industry Outlook

According to S&P Global in January 2026, global oil demand is  expected  to  increase  by  1.1  million  barrels  per  day  to 107.2 million barrels per day in 2026, amidst a gradual return to normalcy in the global economy following the turmoil caused by Trump's reciprocal tariffs in 2025 (normalization of economic conditions).  Meanwhile, global oil  supply,  particularly  from non-OPEC+ countries in Latin America such as Brazil and Guyana, is expected to remain high. Supply from OPEC+ countries will require close monitoring to determine the balance between supporting oil prices and reclaiming market share. The average price of Dubai crude oil in 2026 is projected to  be  between USD 60 - 70 per barrel. The Singapore cracking margin for 2026 is expected to average USD 4.4 - 5.4 per barrel.

The overall price of petrochemical products is projected to decline in 2026, amidst continued economic expansion and lower crude oil and naphtha prices. New HDPE supply is planned to continuously enter the market in 2026 from both Asia  and  the  U.S.  (which  has  a  cost  advantage  and  may intensify price competition), while new PX supply entering the market in 2026 is expected to be relatively limited. The HDPE average price in 2026 is projected to be between USD 830 - 860 per ton, and the PX average price USD 800 - 840 per ton.

## 2.3  Thailand Economic Outlook

The Thai economy is projected to slow down in 2026. Merchandise exports are expected to experience a significant slowdown due to the high base in 2025 and the impact of Trump's reciprocal tariffs. Furthermore, the government transition after the election may delay the 2027 annual budget process, affecting  public  investment  disbursement.  Private investment, however, is expected to accelerate due to increased investment following investment promotion and the Thailand FastPass program. The number of foreign tourists, particularly Chinese, is expected to gradually recover. Nevertheless, the  U.S.  economic  and  trade  policies,  especially  the  issue of transshipment tariffs and geopolitical conflicts that could lead to higher energy prices, need to be closely monitored. The NESDC in February 2026 forecasted Thailand's economic growth rate at 2.0% (1.5 - 2.5%). For the Thai baht in 2026, analysts predict an average exchange rate of THB 31.0 - 32.0 per USD. Close monitoring of the Fed's easing monetary policy to lower interest rates, Thailand's current account surplus, and movements in the capital account balance is crucial.

## 2.4 Thailand Petroleum Industry Outlook

Thailand's petroleum consumption in 2026 is projected to  remain  stable  or  increase  slightly  compared  to  2025. Aviation fuel consumption is expected to continue increasing due to the resurgence of international tourism, both short-haul and long-haul. Diesel consumption is expected to increase slightly from a low base in 2025, while gasoline consumption is anticipated to benefit from lower global oil  prices,  but  geopolitical  conflicts  and  the  rise  of  electric vehicles need to be monitored. Fuel oil consumption is expected to slow down from a high base in 2025 as the front-loading effects fade. LPG consumption is expected to decrease slightly  due  to  low  oil  prices,  leading  to  a  switch back to gasoline.

## 1.  Structure and Business Operations of PTT Group

## 1.1   Business Policy and Overview

## Background

The Petroleum Authority of Thailand was established on December 29, 1978, according to the Petroleum Authority of Thailand Act B.E. 2521, principally to operate petroleum and related businesses. However, amidst an intense competition environment, the Government recognized the necessity of the  private  sector's  participation  in  the  energy  business. The Cabinet resolution on August 21, 2001 approved a corporatization plan of the Petroleum Authority of Thailand, and on September 25, 2001 approved the corporatization under the State Enterprise Corporatization Act B.E. 2542 to  establish  PTT  Plc.  (PTT)  by  converting  the  capital  of the Petroleum Authority of Thailand into capital stock. It is mandated that the state holds at least 51% share in PTT, and for  PTT to remain the national oil company. After the Civil administration reform, PTT was placed under the supervision of the Ministry of Energy.

On October 1, 2001, the Petroleum Authority of Thailand became PTT Plc. under the State  Enterprise  Corporatization  Act  with  an  initial  registered  capital  of  THB  20,000 million, divided into 2,000 million ordinary shares at a par value of THB 10, with the Ministry of Finance as the sole shareholder 1/ . Subsequently, shares were offered to the public and institutional investors and then traded in the Stock Exchange of Thailand on December 6, 2001. The Finance Ministry remained a majority shareholder, and PTT's status remains that of a state enterprise regulated by the Ministry of Energy to this day. (On April 24, 2018, PTT split the par value from THB 10 to THB 1, resulting in  a  total  number  of  28,562.9  million  common  shares,  and  registered  capital  of THB 28,562.9 million.)
